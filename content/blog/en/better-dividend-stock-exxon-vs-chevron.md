This is the most common head-to-head in energy income investing, and most articles settle it with vibes. We settled it with data: our five-pillar model grades, every dividend payment both companies made since 2008 in our database, and price replays of two real market crashes.

## The scorecard

| | ExxonMobil (XOM) | Chevron (CVX) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Model grade | **C+** | **C** |
| Dividend yield (TTM) | ~2.8% | **~3.7%** |
| 2025 calendar dividend | $4.00/share | $6.84/share |
| Dividend growth, 5yr | ~2.8%/yr | **~5.8%/yr** |
| Calendar years of growth (2008–2025, verified) | 17/17 | 17/17 |
| 2022 inflation shock | **+54.9%** | +32.2% |
| 2025 tariff shock | **−8.6%** | −12.9% |

## What the payment records show

We summed every payment in our database by calendar year. Both companies show **a higher total every single year from 2008 through 2025** — through the 2014–16 oil bust and through COVID, when oil futures briefly traded negative. (The companies' own declared-rate streaks are longer — roughly four decades each — our 17-year figure is what we can independently verify payment by payment.)

The texture differs, though. Exxon's totals from 2019 to 2021 were $3.43 → $3.48 → $3.49 — penny raises to keep the streak technically alive while the business bled. Chevron raised from $4.76 to $5.31 over the same stretch and never slowed: $5.68, $6.04, $6.52, $6.84 since. Over the last five years Chevron's payout compounded at **~5.8% per year against Exxon's ~2.8%** — at those rates, Chevron's income stream doubles roughly a decade sooner.

## What the crash replays show

Energy was the *only* hedge that worked in 2022 — but not equally. In the inflation-shock window (Jan–Oct 2022, S&P 500 −25.4%), **Exxon gained +54.9% to Chevron's +32.2%**. In the 2025 tariff shock (S&P −19.0%), Exxon fell −8.6% to Chevron's −12.9%. Two crises, two different regimes, same ranking — the market has consistently treated Exxon as the sturdier asset, which is partly why it now yields a full point less.

## The verdict

**Buying an income stream? Chevron.** A ~3.7% starting yield growing ~5.8% a year beats ~2.8% growing ~2.8% a year on every income-math horizon that matters.

**Buying energy exposure that won't wreck your portfolio in a panic? Exxon.** It outperformed in both measured crises, and its premium valuation is the price of that sturdiness.

**Buying "the best dividend stock"? Neither, honestly.** C+ and C are middling grades — the model currently finds better risk-adjusted income elsewhere (see our verified [buy-and-hold list](/blog/best-dividend-stocks-to-buy-and-hold-forever)). Energy belongs in an income portfolio as a slice, not a centerpiece — and in our replays both names moved the same direction in every crisis, so owning both diversifies far less than it feels like it does.
