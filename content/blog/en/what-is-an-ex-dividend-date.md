The **ex-dividend date** is the single most important date for dividend investors: it determines whether *you* or the *previous owner* receives the next payout.

## The four dividend dates

| Date | What happens |
| --- | --- |
| **Declaration date** | The board announces the dividend and its amount |
| **Ex-dividend date** | The cut-off — buy *before* this date to receive the dividend |
| **Record date** | The company checks its books for eligible shareholders (usually 1 business day after the ex-date) |
| **Payment date** | The cash is deposited into your brokerage account |

## The simple rule

To collect a dividend, **you must own the stock before its ex-dividend date.** Buy on the ex-date itself — or after — and you miss this round; the dividend goes to whoever sold you the shares.

Because settlement is near-instant for eligibility purposes, the ex-date is what matters, not the record date. Focus on the ex-date when timing a purchase.

## Why the price drops on the ex-date

On the morning of the ex-dividend date a stock usually opens lower by about the dividend amount. That's normal: the company is about to pay out cash, so the shares are worth slightly less without the right to that payout. This is also why chasing a dividend by buying right before the ex-date and selling right after rarely produces free money — the price adjustment offsets the dividend.
