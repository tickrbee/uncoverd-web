## What Is the Ex-Dividend Date?

The ex-dividend date is the cutoff date for eligibility to receive a shareholder dividend. The buyer of shares **on or after** that date will not be paid the pending dividend.

On that date the stock is said to trade "ex-dividend," meaning its price reflects the upcoming payment. There are four dates to know with stock dividends: the **declaration date**, the **ex-dividend date**, the **record date**, and the **payable date**.

## Understanding the four dividend dates

| Date | What happens |
| --- | --- |
| **Declaration date** | The company announces it will pay a dividend, and its amount |
| **Ex-dividend date** | The cutoff — buy *before* this date to receive the dividend |
| **Record date** | The company compiles the list of eligible shareholders (usually one business day after the ex-date) |
| **Payable date** | The dividend is actually paid to eligible shareholders |

**Declaration date.** The company announces that it will issue a dividend in the future.

**Record date.** The company examines its list of shareholders to determine who will receive the dividend. Only those registered as shareholders on the books as of the record date are entitled to it.

**Ex-dividend date.** This determines which shareholders are entitled to the dividend, and is typically set one business day before the record date. Shareholders who bought on the ex-date or after will not receive the dividend; those who owned the shares at least one full business day before will.

**Payable date.** Also called the payment date — when the dividend actually reaches eligible shareholders' accounts. It is always the last of the four dates.

## Ex-dividend date and the stock price

Many investors want to buy before the ex-dividend date to be sure they receive the upcoming dividend. But if you buy just after and realize you missed it, you may not have lost as much as you think.

Share prices usually **drop by about the dividend amount** on the ex-dividend date, because the company's assets are about to decline by that payout. If a company announces a dividend worth 2% of its stock price, the stock may fall ~2% on the ex-date — so a buyer on or shortly after the ex-date effectively gets a ~2% "discount" versus buying just before.

> **Important:** Because stocks usually decline by about the dividend on the ex-dividend date, investors who missed buying beforehand can often pick up the stock at a discount roughly equal to the dividend on or after the ex-date.

## Example of an ex-dividend date

Consider a company that declares a dividend on **Tuesday, July 30**. If the record date is **Thursday, Aug 8**, the ex-dividend date is **Wednesday, Aug 7** — anyone buying on Aug 7 or later does not receive the dividend. A shareholder who bought on **Tuesday, Aug 6** (or earlier) is entitled to it. The payable date here is **Friday, Sept 6**.

| Declaration date | Ex-dividend date | Record date | Payable date |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Tue, Jul 30 | Wed, Aug 7 | Thu, Aug 8 | Fri, Sept 6 |

## Is it better to buy before or after the ex-dividend date?

Buying before means you receive the dividend; buying after means you typically get the stock at a price already reduced by about the dividend. Neither is automatically "better" — the market adjusts for the payout either way.

## The bottom line

If you're investing for dividends, timing matters: **buy before the ex-dividend date**, and you can sell on the ex-date or later and still receive that dividend. If you buy after, you'll usually benefit from the price adjustment that factors the dividend in.
