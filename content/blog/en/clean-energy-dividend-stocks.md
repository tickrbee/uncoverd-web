Developed-world electricity demand barely moved for two decades, as efficiency gains cancelled out growth. That has changed. AI data centres, heat pumps replacing gas boilers, and electric vehicles are pulling on the grid at once, and utilities from Texas to Germany have raised their demand forecasts sharply. Data centres are the clearest example: they run continuously, and their owners increasingly insist the power be carbon-free.

Three forces sit underneath the theme, and each now rests on economics rather than policy.

1. **Cost.** Utility-scale solar and onshore wind are the cheapest source of *new* generation in most markets, even without subsidy, on [Lazard's levelised cost of energy work](https://www.lazard.com/research-insights/levelized-cost-of-energyplus/). The case rests on the bill, not the grant.
2. **Sovereignty.** Since 2022, domestic generation has become a security question across Europe, where imported gas proved a strategic weakness. The lesson is reinforced whenever shipping is threatened at the Strait of Hormuz — the chokepoint for roughly a fifth of the world's oil and much of its LNG. Power generated at home cannot be blockaded.
3. **Demand for firm power.** Hyperscalers are contracting directly with generators, and the [IEA expects data-centre electricity use to roughly double by 2030](https://www.iea.org/energy-system/buildings/data-centres-and-data-transmission-networks). That has revived nuclear and grid investment written off a decade ago.

## Why the manufacturers are a value trap

The hardware side of clean energy — polysilicon, cells, modules, wind turbines — has been hollowed out by Chinese overcapacity. China now controls more than 80% of every stage of the global solar supply chain, per the [IEA's analysis of solar PV manufacturing](https://www.iea.org/reports/solar-pv-global-supply-chains), and the price collapse that followed has erased the margins of Western makers. A thematic "clean energy" screen surfaces exactly these companies, often on headline yields the cash flows cannot support.

The durable businesses sit one step removed: the operators that buy cheap hardware and sell power under contract, and the firms that build the grid the whole build-out depends on. That is the divide the ratings below capture.

## The picks, by role

Each of the six below earns its grade for a different reason. You can see every clean-energy name with its uncoverd grade on the **[Clean Energy stock list →](/industries/clean-energy)**.

**The grid and turbine builder — GE Vernova (GEV).** Rated **B**, up about 99% over the past year, with a token ~0.1% dividend. Spun out of GE, it spans Power (gas, nuclear, hydro, steam), Wind, and the Electrification equipment the AI build-out is bottlenecked on. Its growth and share-price momentum run well ahead of other large power names — but the market knows it, and the stock is among the more expensive in its group, with profitability and balance sheet around the middle of the pack. In plain terms: the clearest way to own the grid-and-turbine build-out, already priced for it. You are buying quality and momentum, not a bargain.

**The round-the-clock renewable — Ormat Technologies (ORA).** The highest grade here, **B+**, up about 90% on the year. Ormat runs geothermal and storage across the US, Indonesia, Kenya, Turkey and Chile, and geothermal is the rare renewable that generates baseload power continuously — exactly the profile data centres will sign contracts for. What sets it apart is balance: the strongest share-price momentum in its peer group and faster growth than most, yet it still does not trade at a premium, which is unusual for a top scorer. Its only soft spot is that margins and balance sheet are about average for the group. It is the one name that pairs strong growth and momentum without asking you to overpay.

**The nuclear-for-AI play — Constellation Energy (CEG).** Rated **B**, on a ~0.6% yield, and down about 9% over the year — the pullback is the interesting part. Constellation is the largest US producer of carbon-free electricity, overwhelmingly nuclear, and nuclear's reliability has made it the supply of choice for hyperscaler power deals. On the two things that matter most for a company selling power under contract — fat margins and a strong balance sheet — it is the highest-quality name of the group. What holds the grade at B is price action: it is the weakest performer here over the past year. Best balance sheet, worst recent momentum — the whole debate sits in one stock.

**The blue-chip developer — NextEra Energy (NEE).** Rated **B** on a **~2.6% yield**, the highest among the growth-tilted names here. NextEra bolts the world's largest wind-and-solar developer onto a regulated Florida utility, so it is judged against regulated utilities rather than pure renewables. Against that yardstick it reads as solid but unspectacular: better margins than its regulated peers, and everything else middle-of-the-road. The market still pays a premium for it, and its balance sheet carries a little more debt than peers — the long-running question over the group's leverage. It remains the closest thing to a one-stop, dividend-paying way to own the renewable build-out at scale.

**The income yieldcos — Clearway (CWEN) and Brookfield Renewable (BEPC).** Both pass contracted cash to shareholders, and the grades correct a common assumption. **Clearway**, on a **~4.3% yield**, is rated **B**: it is the cheaper of the two relative to peers, with otherwise average fundamentals — you are paid ~4.3% to own operating wind and solar. **Brookfield Renewable** pays more, a **~4.8% yield**, but grades lower at **C+** — and the reason is not profitability, where Brookfield's margins are actually the stronger of the two. It is that Brookfield is the most expensive name in its group, grows more slowly, and carries more debt. The extra yield comes with a higher price tag and a weaker balance sheet — exactly what the lower grade is flagging.

## Where the rating earns its keep

Clean energy is the sector where a high yield most often signals high risk, and where the obvious thematic buys — the panel and turbine makers — carry the worst fundamentals. Grading each company on value, quality, growth, momentum and balance-sheet strength, against its own peers, is what separates Ormat and Constellation from a stretched manufacturer or an over-leveraged yieldco. The grade does not tell you the theme is real; the demand and cost data already do that. It tells you which companies are positioned to turn the theme into cash.
