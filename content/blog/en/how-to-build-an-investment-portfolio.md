Most people build portfolios backwards: they start with "which stock should I buy?" — which is step four. Get the first three steps right and the stock picking becomes the easy part. Here's the order professionals actually follow.

## Step 1: Define the job of the money

A portfolio is a tool for a job: retirement in 25 years, a house deposit in 5, income from next year. The job sets the **horizon**, and the horizon sets how much volatility you can afford to ignore. One honest question matters more than any quiz: *how far can this fall before I sell in panic?* That number — your real drawdown tolerance — is the foundation everything else stands on.

## Step 2: Set the asset mix

The split between **stocks, bonds and cash** drives the vast majority of your portfolio's behavior. Rough anchors: aggressive (long horizon, strong stomach) ~90/10 stocks/bonds; balanced ~70/25/5; conservative ~50/40/10. Bonds aren't there for excitement — they're there so the bad year is −15% instead of −35%, which keeps you invested.

## Step 3: Diversify by correlation, not by count

Twenty tech stocks are one bet wearing twenty costumes. Real diversification means assets that **move differently**: sectors with different cycles, some international exposure, bonds against equity shocks. The measure that matters is the **correlation matrix** — pairs near 1.0 are the same risk twice; pairs near zero are the free lunch. Aim for an "effective holdings" number close to your actual count.

## Step 4: Pick the instruments

Now — and only now — the names. A proven structure is **core and satellites**: a broad, low-cost index ETF as the core (the market's return, guaranteed), with **quality-rated stocks** as satellites for income or conviction. ## Step 5: Size positions by risk

Two rules prevent most disasters. **Cap single positions** around 10–12% — no thesis is good enough to bet the portfolio on. And check **risk contribution**: a volatile 5% position can drive 15% of your total risk. Size so that no single name dominates the risk budget, not just the dollar budget.

## Step 6: Stress test before you commit

Before funding the portfolio, ask what it would have done in 2008, in March 2020, in 2022, in the 2025 tariff shock. If the simulated drawdown exceeds the tolerance you wrote down in step 1, fix it now — more ballast, less concentration — not during the next crash.

## Step 7: Automate and maintain

Contribute monthly (automatic, so the decision is made once), **rebalance** when weights drift far from target (that's selling high and buying low, systematized), and review annually — not daily. The portfolio's worst enemy after launch is its owner's boredom.
