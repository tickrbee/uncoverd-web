"Forever" is the most abused word in dividend investing. Every list promises it; almost none defines it. Ours does: a **verified multi-decade raise streak** — measured from actual payments in our database, which reaches back to 1970 — **plus a current uncoverd grade of B or better**. The streak proves the culture; the grade proves the culture still has fuel.

## Who's famous but didn't make it

This is the part other lists won't print. **Coca-Cola, PepsiCo, Target, Exxon, ADP and Colgate all currently grade C+** in our model — their streaks live on, but valuation, momentum or profitability has cooled. **Chevron and AbbVie sit at C.** None of these are broken businesses; they're businesses you'd be paying yesterday's reputation for today. A streak describes the past. The grade describes the present. Forever needs both.

One methodology note for the sharp-eyed: we count **cash actually paid per calendar year**, which can differ from official declared-rate streaks when a company shifts a payment across the year boundary — it's why our measured KO and PG streaks read shorter than their official 60+ year status. We prefer measuring money over measuring press releases.

## What "forever" actually requires of you

**A boring superpower: the annual checkup.** Buy-and-hold doesn't mean buy-and-ignore. Once a year, check three things: the grade (has the model's view deteriorated?), the payout ratio (is the dividend outrunning earnings?), and the raise itself (a freeze after decades of growth is the loudest warning a board can give). Every name above has a live rating page — JNJ's grade today may not be JNJ's grade in 2030, and that's precisely the point of checking.

**Diversify even your convictions.** Ten forever stocks from this list span healthcare, industrials, utilities, materials, consumer and retail — that's deliberate.
