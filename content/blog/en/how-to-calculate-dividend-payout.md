Working out how much a dividend pays — and whether that payout is safe — comes down to three simple formulas. This guide walks through each with worked examples, then points you to tools that do the math automatically.

## The three numbers that matter

| Metric | Formula | What it tells you |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Dividend per share (DPS)** | Total dividends ÷ shares outstanding | The cash you receive per share |
| **Dividend yield** | Annual dividend per share ÷ share price | Your income as a % of price |
| **Payout ratio** | Dividends per share ÷ earnings per share | How much profit funds the dividend |

## 1. Dividend per share

If a company pays out **$500 million** in dividends and has **250 million shares outstanding**, the dividend per share is:

`$500,000,000 ÷ 250,000,000 = $2.00 per share`

To find what *you* receive, multiply by your share count. Owning 300 shares at $2.00/year = **$600 per year**.

## 2. Dividend yield

Yield expresses the dividend as a percentage of the current price. A $2.00 annual dividend on a stock trading at $50:

`$2.00 ÷ $50 = 0.04 = 4.0% yield`

When the price falls, the yield rises — which is why an unusually high yield is often a warning sign rather than a bargain. Always check whether the dividend is *covered* before chasing yield.

## 3. Payout ratio (the safety check)

The payout ratio shows how much of a company's earnings are paid out as dividends:

`Dividends per share ÷ earnings per share`

If a company earns **$4.00 per share** and pays **$2.00**, the payout ratio is **50%** — half of profits returned, half retained. A ratio creeping toward 100% means there's little buffer if earnings dip.
