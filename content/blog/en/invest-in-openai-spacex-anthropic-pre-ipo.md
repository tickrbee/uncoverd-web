OpenAI, SpaceX, Anthropic and Databricks are four of the most valuable companies on Earth — and you can't buy a single share of any of them through your brokerage. They're private. That hasn't dimmed investor demand, and a small set of ETFs has stepped in to offer indirect, partial exposure. Here's the honest, **data-backed** guide to **how to invest in OpenAI, SpaceX and Anthropic before the IPO** — which funds actually hold them, how much, and what can go wrong.

## The catch: these companies are private

A public company has shares trading on an exchange anyone can buy. These four don't. Their shares sit on tightly controlled private registers held by employees, venture funds and a few large institutions. There's **no ticker to buy**, and none of them has confirmed an IPO date.

So the truthful answer to "how do I buy OpenAI stock" is: you can't, directly. What you *can* do is buy a fund that owns a piece of it. Here's what's actually in those funds, straight from our holdings data.

### OpenAI
The most direct retail route is the [ARK Innovation ETF (ARKK)](/etfs/symbol/ARKK), which holds an **"OpenAI Group PBC Series C"** position at roughly **2.4% of the fund (~$175M)**. The [ARK Venture Fund (ARKVX)](/etfs/symbol/ARKVX) also holds OpenAI. Note what you're buying: a broad, actively-managed innovation fund that *includes* some OpenAI — not OpenAI itself.

### Anthropic
This one is easy to miss. In our data, the **[KraneShares Artificial Intelligence & Technology ETF (AGIX)](/etfs/symbol/AGIX)** holds **"Anthropic, PBC"** at about **1% of the fund (~$10M)**. AGIX is currently the clearest listed wrapper carrying an Anthropic stake, alongside its OpenAI-adjacent and SpaceX positions.

### SpaceX — and the SPV catch
SpaceX shows up across several funds, but **almost always through an SPV** — a special-purpose vehicle that owns the private shares, with the ETF owning a slice of the SPV:

- **[Tema Space Innovators (NASA)](/etfs/symbol/NASA)** — the heaviest pure exposure, with a SpaceX SPV at **~10% of the fund**.
- **[ERShares Crossover (XOVR)](/etfs/symbol/XOVR)** — a SpaceX SPV at **~9.5%**.
- **[KraneShares (AGIX)](/etfs/symbol/AGIX)** — unusually, holds **both** a SpaceX SPV *and* a small direct **Class-A common** position.
- **[Destiny Tech100 (DXYZ)](/stocks/DXYZ)** — a closed-end fund that has historically held SpaceX and trades all day like a stock.

**Why the SPV detail matters:** an SPV is only marked-to-market periodically, carries its own fee layer, and has its own conversion terms. So the ETF's price **will not move one-for-one, or instantly, with a SpaceX IPO**. If SpaceX listed tomorrow at a big pop, an SPV-based fund might re-rate slowly, partially, and net of fees — not overnight. A small direct common position (like part of AGIX's) tracks more closely than an SPV slice.

### Databricks
For the AI-data layer, the **[Alger AI Enablers & Adopters ETF (ALAI)](/etfs/symbol/ALAI)** holds a **"Databricks Series K" preferred** position at roughly **2% of the fund** — a reminder that "preferred" and "series" shares can carry different rights and payout terms than the common stock you'd get in an IPO.

## How big are these companies, really?

The reason for all the demand: the valuations are staggering. Reported private rounds have put **OpenAI and SpaceX around the half-trillion-dollar mark**, with **Anthropic** and **Databricks** in the hundreds of billions. For scale, a single ~$500B private company rivals the **annual GDP of a mid-sized developed economy** and would rank among the largest members of France's CAC 40 — all before any public investor gets a share. That's the genuinely new feature of this AI cycle: an unprecedented amount of value is being minted *before* the IPO, which is exactly why these wrappers exist. (It's also why this is deepening overall [US market concentration](/blog/us-tech-concentration-magnificent-seven).)

## The risks — read this part twice

Pre-IPO exposure is **speculative**, and the wrappers add risk on top of the companies':

- **SPV lag & opacity.** As above, SPV-held positions are re-valued infrequently and don't track the underlying cleanly.
- **Premium risk.** Closed-end funds like DXYZ have traded at large premiums to the value of their holdings — paying \$3 for \$1 of assets is not "owning SpaceX."
- **Illiquidity.** Interval funds (ARKVX) only allow redemptions periodically; you may not be able to sell when you want.
- **High fees.** Expense ratios here (around 0.55%–1.0%+) dwarf a plain index fund.
- **No IPO guarantee.** "Pre-IPO" assumes an IPO. Some never come; private marks can be cut sharply between rounds.

Sensible framing: treat any pre-IPO position as a **small, speculative slice** you could afford to see fall hard. The [U.S. SEC](https://www.investor.gov) has repeatedly warned investors to be cautious with pre-IPO and private-share offers for exactly these reasons.
