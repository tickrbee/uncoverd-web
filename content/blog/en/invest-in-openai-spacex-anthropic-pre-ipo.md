OpenAI, SpaceX and Anthropic are three of the most valuable companies on Earth — and you can't buy a single share of any of them through your brokerage. They're private. That hasn't stopped enormous investor demand, and a small set of vehicles has sprung up to meet it. Here's the honest guide to what's actually possible, what it costs, and what can go wrong.

## The catch: these companies are private

A public company has shares trading on an exchange that anyone can buy. OpenAI, SpaceX and Anthropic don't. Their shares sit on tightly controlled private registers, available mostly to employees, venture funds and a handful of large institutions. There is **no ticker to buy**, and none of the three has confirmed an IPO date.

So when you see "how to buy OpenAI stock," the truthful answer is: you can't buy it directly. What you *can* do is buy something that owns a piece of it.

## The realistic routes for retail investors

### 1. Destiny Tech100 (DXYZ)
[Destiny Tech100](/stocks/DXYZ) is a closed-end fund that holds stakes in private tech companies — historically including SpaceX and OpenAI — and trades publicly all day like a stock. That's its appeal: instant, liquid access from a normal brokerage account.

The catch is the **premium to NAV**. Because demand for private-AI exposure is so intense and the fund is small, DXYZ has at times traded at *several times* the actual value of its underlying holdings. Paying \$3 for \$1 of assets is a very different proposition from "owning SpaceX." Always compare the share price to the fund's stated net asset value before buying.

### 2. ARK Venture Fund (ARKVX)
The [ARK Venture Fund](/etfs/symbol/ARKVX) is an **interval fund** that has held positions in OpenAI, Anthropic, SpaceX and other private names. Unlike DXYZ, you buy and sell at net asset value, so you don't pay a market premium — but it's **illiquid by design**: redemptions are only offered periodically (typically quarterly) and can be capped. You may not be able to sell exactly when you want.

### 3. Mutual funds that hold SpaceX
Several large active managers — firms like Baron and Fidelity — hold **SpaceX** in mainstream mutual funds. The exposure is diluted (SpaceX might be a low-single-digit percentage of the fund), but these funds are cheap, liquid and don't trade at a premium. A good "get a little exposure without the drama" option.

### 4. Private secondary marketplaces (accredited only)
Platforms such as Forge and EquityZen let **accredited investors** buy existing private shares from employees and early backers. This is the most direct route, but it's gated by accreditation rules, high minimums, lockups and wide bid/ask spreads.

## How big are these companies, really?

The reason for all this demand: the numbers are staggering. Reported private valuations have run into the **hundreds of billions of dollars** for each — OpenAI and SpaceX around the half-trillion mark, Anthropic in the same league. To make that concrete:

- Each of these private companies has been valued at **more than almost any single member of France's CAC 40** — bigger than LVMH, bigger than Hermès, on the reported figures.
- A single one of them, at ~\$500 billion, **rivals the annual GDP of a mid-sized developed economy**.
- Put the three together and you have, on paper, well over **a trillion dollars of value in companies you cannot buy** — created largely in the private market, before any public investor got a look in.

That's the genuinely new thing about this AI cycle: an unprecedented amount of value is being minted *before* the IPO, which is exactly why these indirect vehicles exist.

## The risks — read this part twice

Pre-IPO exposure is **speculative**, and the wrappers add their own risks on top of the companies':

- **Premium risk.** With DXYZ especially, you can overpay massively versus the underlying assets, and that premium can collapse.
- **Illiquidity.** Interval funds and secondaries can trap your money for months. You can't assume you'll sell on demand.
- **Fees.** These vehicles carry far higher expense ratios than a plain index fund.
- **No IPO guarantee.** "Pre-IPO" assumes an IPO. Some never come; valuations can be marked down sharply between funding rounds.
- **Opacity.** Private companies disclose little. You're trusting the fund's marks, not audited public filings.

The sensible framing: treat any pre-IPO position as a **small, speculative slice** of a portfolio you could afford to see fall hard — not a core holding.

If the goal is "exposure to the AI build-out" rather than these specific logos, the public market already offers plenty — and with real financials you can analyze. The companies *supplying* OpenAI and friends — chipmakers, cloud providers, data-center and power names — are listed, liquid and some even pay dividends.
