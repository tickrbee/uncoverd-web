Chasing yield is how dividend investors get hurt; refusing all high yields is how they stay poor slowly. So we did this properly: we screened every US-listed common stock with a market cap above $5 billion and a trailing yield above 5%, kept **only those our model still grades B or better**, then pulled every individual payment from our database to make sure the yield is real. About half the screen's raw output didn't survive that last step — more on that below, because it's the most useful part.

Three names deserve comment. **Altria is the only A-range grade in the entire 5%+ universe** — the model's pillars (cash profitability, balance sheet, valuation against peers) keep scoring it well even as the market prices terminal decline; that argument has paid ~6% a year while people have it. **Pfizer at ~6.6%** is a large-cap pharma paying a steady, verified $0.43 quarterly while the market mourns the post-COVID revenue cliff. And the two food names — **Conagra near 10.4%** and **General Mills above 7%** — are there because consumer-staples share prices have been crushed, not because the payments wobbled: Conagra has paid exactly $0.35 every quarter through the drawdown, and General Mills *raised* to $0.61 in mid-2025. A double-digit yield from a B-graded packaged-food company is the market saying it doubts the payout can survive shrinking sales. The grade says the balance sheet gives it a real chance. That's the bet, stated honestly — size it like a bet.

## How high-yield screens lie to you

We ran the same raw screen every yield site runs, and then checked each payment. Here is what the raw output *also* claimed, and why it was wrong — verified against the actual payment records:

- **Progressive (PGR): "6.9% yield."** Reality: a $0.10 regular quarterly — a 0.4% yield — plus a $13.60 one-time variable payout in January. The "6.9%" repeats only if next year's variable special matches. It's set by formula annually; it is not a commitment.
- **TransDigm (TDG): "7.2% yield."** Reality: a single $90 special dividend and **no regular dividend whatsoever**. Buy it today and your contractual yield is zero.
- **Sanofi (SNY): "10.5% yield."** Reality: an annual payer whose payment dates landed twice inside a 366-day trailing window. Real yield ≈ 5.5% — fine, but half what the screen claims.
- **Old Republic (ORI): "9.6% yield."** Reality: $0.315 regular quarterly (~3.2% run-rate) plus a $2.50 special. Decent company, but the screen-quoted yield triples the recurring rate.
- **Baby bonds everywhere.** Nearly half our raw screen results weren't common stock at all — they were exchange-traded notes and preferreds (tickers like JSM, ATHS, CMSA) that inherit the parent company's listing data. A "7% B-grade stock" that's actually a note has no dividend growth, ever.

This is also why we publish run-rate yields (current payment × frequency ÷ price) rather than raw trailing sums whenever the two disagree.

## The traps the quality filter caught

The raw 5%+ universe includes yields far higher than anything in our table. **Prospect Capital (~24%, grade C)** and **Orchid Island Capital (~21%)** are the canonical cases: serial payout-cutters where the trailing yield always looks spectacular because the price keeps falling ahead of the next reduction. No B-or-better grade, no list — the model's profitability and health pillars are precisely what those names fail.
