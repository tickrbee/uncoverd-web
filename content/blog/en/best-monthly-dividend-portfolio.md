A dividend that lands **every month** is the closest the stock market gets to a salary. The catch: the monthly-payer universe is small, concentrated in two sectors, and hides some of the market's most seductive yield traps. So we built the portfolio the verified way — every name below actually paid **11–12 times between June 2025 and May 2026** in our payment database, and every stock carries a current uncoverd grade.

## Why monthly payers exist at all

Companies pay monthly when their *income* is monthly. **REITs** collect rent every month; **BDCs** collect loan interest every month; **covered-call ETFs** collect option premium every week or month. That's why you won't find a monthly-paying industrial conglomerate — and why a monthly portfolio needs deliberate balancing, because its natural habitat is just two sectors plus fund wrappers.

## The wrappers: monthly income ETFs

The ETF layer adds yield and diversification beyond two sectors. Verified twelve-payment names in our data include **JEPQ (10.7%)**, **SPYI (12.1%)**, **QQQI (14.0%)**, **QYLD (11.8%)** and **XYLD (10.7%)**. They all share one mechanism — selling options against an equity index — and one trade-off: capped upside and distributions that can include return of capital. Used as a slice, they smooth the income; used as the whole portfolio, they cap your wealth. And don't forget the boring sleeve: **bond ETFs like BND and AGG pay monthly too** (3.6–3.7%), adding ballast that pays on schedule.

## The yield traps wear monthly clothing

Here's the part most monthly-income articles skip. In the same verified universe sit **Prospect Capital (PSEC): 24.4% yield, C grade** and **Orchid Island (ORC): 21.4% yield**. Both pay monthly, both screen beautifully on yield, and both are structures where the yield is the warning — leveraged mortgage spreads and stressed BDC books. A 20%+ yield isn't income; it's the market pricing a cut. Our [high-yield list](/high-yield) separates fat yields from durable ones — the difference is the whole game.
