SpaceX is scheduled to begin trading on the Nasdaq on **June 12, 2026** under the ticker **SPCX**. At a targeted valuation of roughly **$1.75 trillion** and a raise of about **$75 billion**, it is set to be the largest initial public offering ever — more than twice the size of Saudi Aramco's 2019 debut, which had held the record for seven years.

If you want a piece of it, the mechanics are simpler than the hype suggests. The hard part isn't *how* to buy; it's deciding *which version* of "buying SpaceX" you actually want. There are three, and they carry very different prices and very different risks.

## Decision 1: The IPO price or the open-market price

This is the distinction that trips up most first-time IPO buyers, so it's worth being precise.

The **IPO price** is the figure the underwriters set the night before trading begins — reportedly **$135 per share** for SpaceX, across an offering of roughly 556 million shares. That price goes to institutions and to a slice of retail investors who are allocated shares *before* the stock ever trades.

The **opening price** is whatever the market decides on the morning of June 12. The two are rarely the same. When a heavily oversubscribed company opens, the first public trade can land well above the IPO price — that gap is the "pop" you read about. The opposite happens too: plenty of hyped offerings have opened flat or below their IPO price and never recovered.

The practical takeaway: getting in at $135 is structurally cheaper than buying at the open, but allocation is not guaranteed, and an allocation is not a gift. You're being invited to buy the same risk everyone else is buying, slightly earlier.

## Decision 2: Which broker — and whether you even qualify

A handful of major brokerages are participating in the retail allocation for SPCX, including Robinhood, SoFi, Charles Schwab, Fidelity, and E\*TRADE. Each sets its own eligibility rules, and those rules have loosened noticeably as the listing approaches — Fidelity, for example, cut the account minimum to participate from $500,000 to $2,000, a sign of how aggressively brokers are courting retail demand.

Two things to understand before you submit a request:

1. **A request is not a fill.** You can ask for 100 shares at the IPO price and receive 10, or zero. Oversubscribed deals get rationed, and the most in-demand offerings ration hard.
2. **Read the conditional-offer terms.** Some brokers require you to confirm your order within a tight window after final pricing, or your allocation lapses. Miss the confirmation and you're back to buying at the open.

If you're going the open-market route instead, the choice is straightforward: log in on June 12, search SPCX, and decide between a **market order** (you buy immediately at whatever the price is) and a **limit order** (you buy only if the stock hits a price you name).

A market order guarantees you own the shares but not the price. A limit order guarantees the price but not the shares — if SPCX gaps up and never comes back to your limit, the order simply expires unfilled. On a volatile first trading day, that trade-off is real, not theoretical.

## Decision 3: Direct shares or wrapped exposure

Not everyone wants to own a single, unproven megacap on its first day. There's a middle path.

Some exchange-traded funds already hold SpaceX indirectly through special-purpose vehicles, and several intend to add SPCX once it's public. A space-focused ETF gives you a sliver of SpaceX bundled with other names — rocket-launch, satellite-imagery, and space-infrastructure companies — so a bad week for SpaceX alone doesn't sink your whole position. You can [screen space and thematic ETFs here](/screener).

The trade-off is dilution of upside: if SpaceX doubles and the rest of the basket doesn't, you capture only a fraction of the move. ETF holdings acquired pre-IPO through SPVs are also typically subject to lockups (often six months) before they convert to freely tradable shares, so the fund's "SpaceX exposure" may be less liquid than it looks on paper.

For investors who care more about not getting whipsawed than about maximizing a single bet, wrapped exposure is the calmer entry.

## A quick word on the index effect

There's a structural wrinkle worth knowing. Index providers have fast-tracked SpaceX's eligibility for inclusion — the Nasdaq-100 introduced "Fast Entry" rules letting the largest IPOs join in as few as 15 trading days, and the Russell indexes shortened their window to a handful of sessions. S&P Dow Jones considered a similar change for the S&P 500 but declined.

Why does this matter to you? Because index funds that track those benchmarks will be *forced* to buy SPCX once it's added, regardless of price. That mechanical demand can support the stock in its first weeks. It can also evaporate once the buying is done — so don't mistake index-driven flows for a verdict on the business.

## The actual game plan

Whatever path you choose, decide it *before* June 12, not during the open:

- Know your order type and your maximum price in advance.
- If you requested an IPO allocation, know your broker's confirmation deadline.
- Size the position as if it could fall 30% in week one — because new megacap listings have done exactly that. A quick [Portfolio Healthcheck](/tools/portfolio-healthcheck) shows how a single volatile position changes your overall risk before you buy.
- Be honest about whether you're investing in a business or trying to catch a pop. They require different exit plans.

SpaceX going public is a genuinely historic event. That doesn't make every entry point a good one. The investors who do well on June 12 will mostly be the ones who decided what they wanted on June 11.
