The largest IPO in history lands on the Nasdaq on **June 12, 2026**. SpaceX — now bundled with xAI's Grok and the Starlink network after February's merger — is targeting a **$1.75 trillion** valuation and a **$75 billion** raise at an IPO price of about **$135 a share**.

The headlines frame it as a once-in-a-generation chance for ordinary investors to own a piece of the future. Look at the plumbing underneath, though, and a less flattering picture emerges: much of the machinery around this listing has been arranged so that retail demand absorbs supply at exactly the moment insiders are best positioned to sell. None of this means SpaceX is a bad company. It means the *entry point* is structured in insiders' favor, and that's worth understanding before you click buy.

## The index rules were rewritten to create forced buyers

In the months before the listing, several major index providers changed their methodologies specifically to let a company this large join faster than the rules normally allow.

The Nasdaq-100 introduced a "Fast Entry" provision letting the biggest non-financial IPOs qualify in as little as 15 trading days, instead of the usual multi-month wait. The Russell index series went further, shortening SpaceX's eligibility window to roughly five sessions after the debut. S&P Dow Jones considered a comparable change for the S&P 500 and, notably, decided against it.

Here's why that matters. Every index fund, mutual fund, and 401(k) that tracks those benchmarks will be *obligated* to buy SPCX once it's included — not because a manager judged it cheap, but because the rules require it. That's tens of billions of dollars in price-insensitive demand hitting a relatively thin initial float.

In the short run, that buying can prop the stock up. The problem is what it's propping up *against*.

## Staggered lockups give insiders an early exit

A standard IPO locks insiders out of selling for 180 days. SpaceX's structure is more permissive: some insiders are reportedly able to begin selling shares within days of the company's first quarterly report — likely in August — rather than waiting the full six months. (Elon Musk himself is reported to be excluded from the early window.)

Stack the two facts together and the dynamic becomes clear. Index inclusion manufactures a wave of forced buying. The early lockup expiry hands insiders a door to sell into it. When the entity doing the mandatory buying is a pension fund and the entity doing the selling is an early investor cashing out, retail investors are frequently the marginal buyer on the other side of both — what traders bluntly call *exit liquidity*.

## The valuation asks you to believe a lot

Set the mechanics aside and the price alone demands scrutiny.

SpaceX is a capital-intensive business that is still spending heavily and is not near durable, recurring profitability. Folding in xAI doesn't obviously fix that — the AI unit behind Grok remains a distant third in a race that Anthropic and OpenAI are leading by a wide margin, despite an enormous addressable-market story attached to it.

At a $1.75 trillion valuation against revenue in the low tens of billions, the stock would debut at a price-to-sales multiple in territory that almost no company has sustained for long, even ones that genuinely changed the world. History is unkind here: companies that have carried very high sales multiples have rarely held them for years, and several of the largest, most hyped megacap IPOs of the past two decades traded down by double digits within six months of listing.

A great company can still be a poor investment if you overpay at the entrance. That gap — between *good business* and *good price* — is the entire question with SPCX.

## What a clear-eyed investor actually does

Skepticism isn't the same as avoidance. There are reasonable ways to engage without walking into the setup blind:

- **Separate the company from the trade.** SpaceX may well be a dominant business for decades. That is a different claim from "buying at $135 on day one is a good entry."
- **Don't confuse mechanical flows for conviction.** Index-driven buying is not the market endorsing the valuation. It's funds following rules. When the forced buying finishes, that support can fade.
- **Watch the August lockup.** If you're holding, the first early-lockup expiry is a real supply event, not a footnote.
- **Size for a drawdown.** Treat a 30%+ first-quarter decline as a plausible outcome, not a tail risk. A [Portfolio Healthcheck](/tools/portfolio-healthcheck) shows what one volatile position does to your overall risk before you commit.
- **Consider waiting.** There is no rule that says you must own a stock on its first day. Letting the index buying, the first earnings print, and the initial lockup pass can hand you a far clearer picture — and often a better price. If income and durability matter more to you, [higher-rated dividend names](/high-yield) are a calmer place to start.

The most valuable thing to remember about June 12 is whose interests the structure serves. The rule changes, the loosened broker minimums, the accelerated lockups — each of them increases the supply of SpaceX shares flowing toward retail at the debut. That's not a conspiracy; it's how a $75 billion sale gets done. Knowing it is the difference between participating on your own terms and being the exit on someone else's.
