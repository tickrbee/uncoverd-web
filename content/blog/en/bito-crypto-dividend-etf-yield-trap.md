Sort any ETF screener by yield and the top of the list is never a dividend fund — it's a derivatives fund wearing a dividend costume. The reigning champion is **BITO, the ProShares Bitcoin Strategy ETF**, which screens at a trailing yield of roughly **70%**. We verified what a holder actually experienced over the last twelve months, payment by payment, against daily closing prices from our database. It's the cleanest yield-trap case study we've ever published.

## The verified math

| | Last 12 months (verified) |
| --- | --- |
| Distributions paid | **$6.01** per share, across 11 monthly payments |
| Share price, start | $21.79 |
| Share price, now | $8.64 (**−60.3%**) |
| Total return incl. all distributions | **−32.8%** |
| "Yield" a screener displays today | $6.01 ÷ $8.64 ≈ **69.5%** |

Read that last row carefully, because it explains the whole genre: the advertised yield is *trailing distributions divided by the current price*. The price collapsed 60%, so the displayed yield **went up**. The worse the fund does, the more irresistible it looks in a screen. A yield-sorted screener is literally ranking funds by a blend of payout size and price damage.

## Where the "dividend" actually comes from

No scandal, just mechanics. BITO holds bitcoin *futures*, and rolling those contracts realizes gains the fund must distribute to keep its tax status. Every distribution mechanically reduces the fund's NAV by the same amount on the ex-date — the money doesn't come from earnings, because there are none; **it comes out of the share price you own**. When bitcoin rises, that's fine: you receive your own gains in monthly installments (and owe tax on each one). When bitcoin falls — as it did over this window — the machine keeps distributing anyway, shrinking your base while the screener congratulates the fund on its rising yield.

The same arithmetic governs the broader crypto-income genre — covered-call bitcoin funds and single-stock option-income ETFs — where eye-popping distribution rates coexist with deeply negative total returns. The distribution rate is a *withdrawal schedule*, not a yield.

## The test that catches every fund like this

Ask one question: **would the total return have funded the payout?** A real dividend payer — say, [Altria at ~5.9% with an A− grade](/blog/safest-ultra-high-yield-dividend-stocks) — pays from operating cash flow that exists whether or not you look at the share price. BITO's twelve-month total return was −32.8%; nothing funded those distributions except the holders' own capital and a falling bitcoin price. That's the line between income investing and a monthly liquidation plan.
