Every "live off dividends" article eventually shows you the same fantasy: a double-digit yield that makes the required capital look small. We'll show you the real menu instead — four verified yield levels from our own database, what each costs in capital, and what each costs in risk.

$1,000 a month is $12,000 a year. Capital needed = **$12,000 ÷ yield**. That's the whole formula; everything else is choosing the yield honestly.

## The four rungs, verified

| Strategy | Yield (verified) | Capital needed | What can go wrong |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Dividend ETF (SCHD) | 3.25% | **$369,000** | Least single-name risk; most capital |
| Quality blend, B+ grades | ~5.0% | **$240,000** | Single names; modest cut risk |
| High-yield, B-or-better | ~6.5% | **$185,000** | Real cut risk, rate sensitivity |
| Aggressive monthly payers | ~7.8% | **$154,000** | Includes names priced for cuts |

The rungs are real portfolios, not hypotheticals. The 3.25% is [SCHD's verified trailing yield](/blog/schd-vs-vym-vs-dgro). The ~5% blend is buildable from our [safest ultra-high-yield list](/blog/safest-ultra-high-yield-dividend-stocks) — Altria (A−, ~5.9%), Realty Income (B, ~5.2%, monthly), Verizon (B, ~5.9%) cut with lower-yielding quality. The ~6.5% tier leans on Pfizer (~6.6%), General Mills (~7.2%) and peers. The 7.8% is the [monthly-payer blend we verified payment by payment](/blog/best-monthly-dividend-portfolio) — and that article is honest that some of its yield comes from names like Prospect Capital, where a 24% trailing yield is the market quoting you the odds of a cut.

Between the top and bottom rung sits **$215,000 of capital — or, read the other way, a doubling of risk**. That's the actual decision. Anyone who only shows you the $154k number is selling the risk without pricing it.

## The shortcut nobody markets: growth + time

A 5% blend isn't static — the names behind it raise payouts. At 5% dividend growth, income doubles roughly every 14 years; with dividends reinvested along the way it's much faster. Practically: **$240,000 isn't the entry fee, it's the finish line.** Invest $170k at a 5% yield growing 5%/year and reinvest everything, and you cross $1,000/month in roughly seven years without adding new money. ## Three honest footnotes

1. **Taxes.** $1,000 gross ≈ $850 net of US qualified-dividend tax for a median taxable filer; in an IRA it's $1,000. Non-US readers: wrappers change everything — French investors, see [€500/month via the PEA](/fr/blog/500-euros-dividende-par-mois).
2. **Payment calendars aren't monthly by default.** Most quarterly payers cluster in Mar/Jun/Sep/Dec; "$1,000 a month" usually means "$3,000 a quarter" unless you sequence payers deliberately — the [monthly portfolio article](/blog/best-monthly-dividend-portfolio) shows verified payment schedules.
3. **Principal risk is income risk.** In 2022, "safe" Verizon fell 32% — see the [crash replays](/blog/recession-resistant-dividend-stocks). Yield on a shrinking base is how income plans die.
