One day, one announcement, a quarter of the company's value gone. **Super Micro Computer (NASDAQ: SMCI)** was the S&P 500's worst performer on June 10, 2026 — and unusually, the math of the fall is almost clean enough to do on a napkin.

## The move

SMCI fell **−26.90% to $29.71** from a previous close of $40.64, on a huge 138.7M shares. It opened down at $35.60, slid all day, and **closed at the session low** — the weakest possible tape. The stock now sits at a $17.9B market cap, far below its 52-week high of $62.36 and closer to its $19.48 low than anyone holding since last year wants to see.

## What's driving it

Supermicro said it wants to raise **about $7 billion in new equity** to fund AI-server demand — including the Nvidia-driven workloads that have made its order book explode. The intent is expansion; the immediate effect is **dilution**: $7B of new shares against a ~$24.4B pre-announcement market cap hands roughly **29% of the company** to new investors. The stock fell ~27%. That's not panic — that's arithmetic.

## The fundamentals

uncoverd rates SMCI **B**, with **Growth 4/5** and Value, Profitability, Momentum and Health all at 3/5. The growth is real: the December quarter booked **$12.7B of revenue**, March **$10.2B**. The problem is what reaches the bottom line — **$0.48B of net income on that $10.2B**, a net margin near 4%. A business that converts so little revenue into profit must keep raising capital to grow, which is exactly what today's announcement is.

## Is the crash justified?

**Largely, yes — and the way it's justified matters.** When a fall matches the dilution math, the market is repricing share count, not rejecting the business. But two details add a trust discount on top: the raise comes at a price well below the year's highs (selling cheap equity to fund expensive growth), and the stock closed at the day's low, meaning no buyers stepped in front of the supply. The market's message: prove that $7B of demand becomes profit — margins first, share count second — and the multiple can come back.

## What to watch

The pricing and structure of the offering (equity vs. converts), whether Nvidia-linked order momentum shows up as margin and not just revenue, and the next quarter's gross margin print. If margins inflect upward, today's seller financed someone else's entry point; if they don't, the dilution was the honest price of standing still.
