Most dividend investors get paid four times a year and wait the other 48 weeks. But dividend calendars are staggered — and if you treat the payment schedule as an engineering problem, you can build a portfolio where **some position pays you every single week of the year**. We didn't just theorize: we replayed a year of real payment dates from our database to prove the blueprint below actually does it. Here's the honest version, including the parts the YouTube thumbnails skip.

## The math: three cycles plus monthly payers

US companies that pay quarterly almost all follow one of **three payment cycles**:

- **Cycle A — January, April, July, October.** Classic examples: Altria, JPMorgan, Cisco.
- **Cycle B — February, May, August, November.** Classic examples: Procter & Gamble, AbbVie, Verizon.
- **Cycle C — March, June, September, December.** Classic examples: Johnson & Johnson, Microsoft, ExxonMobil.

Own quality names from all three cycles and you already collect in **every month of the year**. The weekly resolution comes from two more ingredients: **monthly payers**, whose paydays land in different weeks of the month, and the fact that within a cycle, different companies pay in different weeks of the month.

## The backbone: monthly payers

A monthly payer is worth three quarterly payers in calendar terms. The famous one is **Realty Income (O)** — it trademarked "The Monthly Dividend Company" and has paid every month for decades. **Main Street Capital (MAIN)**, **STAG Industrial**, **Agree Realty (ADC)** and **Healthpeak (DOC)** are other established names. Two things to know: most monthly payers are **REITs or BDCs**, so they cluster in two sectors (concentration you must offset elsewhere), and their pay dates differ — Realty Income tends to pay mid-month while others pay at the turn of the month. That difference is exactly what you want.

You can find every monthly payer we cover with the [dividend screener](/screener), and check any name's actual payment history on its uncoverd profile before you rely on it.

## The verified blueprint: 19 positions, 52 out of 52 weeks

We didn't stop at theory. We replayed the **actual payment dates** of this exact book against our dividends database for the twelve months from June 2025 through May 2026. The result: **a dividend landed in all 52 weeks of the year — 133 deposits, never fewer than one per week, 2.6 per week on average.**

| Slot | Role | The verified names |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 5 positions | Monthly payers, offset paydays | Realty Income (O), Main Street (MAIN), STAG, Agree Realty (ADC), Healthpeak (DOC) |
| 3 positions | Cycle A quality payers | Altria (MO), JPMorgan (JPM), Cisco (CSCO) |
| 3 positions | Cycle B quality payers | Procter & Gamble (PG), AbbVie (ABBV), Verizon (VZ) |
| 3 positions | Cycle C quality payers | Johnson & Johnson (JNJ), ExxonMobil (XOM), Microsoft (MSFT) |
| 3 positions | Deliberate gap-fillers | Apollo (APO), Ameriprise (AMP), Analog Devices (ADI) |
| 2 positions | Income ETFs, monthly distribution | JEPI, JEPQ |

The interesting part is the gap-filler row. The first sixteen positions covered 50 weeks but stubbornly missed two — mid-March and the year's final week. **Analog Devices pays in exactly those two weeks**, which is why a semiconductor name sits in an income book: it closes the calendar. Apollo and Ameriprise plug the other off-cycle weeks (weeks 9, 14, 21–22, 34–35 and 48 in our replay).

Two honest footnotes: past pay dates don't guarantee future ones — companies shift paydays, so verify the names you actually buy on the [ex-dividend calendar](/calendar/ex-dividend) — and a one-week slip by a single payer can briefly reopen a gap. The schedule is robust, not contractual.

## The honest catches

**Frequency isn't return.** A 4% yield pays 4% whether it arrives in 52 slices or 4. Anyone selling "weekly dividends" as extra income is selling arithmetic confusion. The genuine benefits are behavioral — regular reinforcement keeps you invested — and practical, if you actually live off the income.

**The calendar must never pick the stock.** A payer that fits week 23 perfectly but cuts its dividend next year pays you on no schedule at all. Filter for quality first — payout ratio, balance sheet, dividend history — then arrange the survivors by calendar. Our [high-yield list](/high-yield) flags the difference between a fat yield and a durable one.

**Weekly-distribution ETFs are a different animal.** In our database, covered-call ETFs like XDTE and QDTE really did distribute in 51–52 weeks of the past year — one ticket solves the whole calendar. But those distributions come from selling options: upside is capped, part of what you receive can be return of capital, and NAV can erode in rough markets. As a slice of the income sleeve they're useful; as the whole portfolio they're a yield trap with a friendly schedule.

**Taxes don't care about your calendar.** REIT and BDC dividends are mostly taxed as ordinary income, and covered-call distributions have their own treatment. The schedule optimizes cash flow, not the tax bill.
