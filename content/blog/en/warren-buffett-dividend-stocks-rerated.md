The most copied portfolio on earth is disclosed four times a year in Berkshire Hathaway's 13F filing, and a small industry exists to repackage it as a dividend strategy. So we did the one thing the repackagers don't: ran Berkshire's best-known holdings through our five-pillar model — value, growth, profitability, momentum, balance-sheet health, scored against industry peers — and verified every dividend against actual payment records. The result says more about *when* Buffett buys than *what* he holds.

Not a single A-range grade. A wall of Bs and C-pluses — solid, fairly-priced businesses — which is roughly what you'd expect when the whole world has spent decades front-running one man's buy list.

## The two stories in the table

**Coca-Cola, C+.** Heresy first: the most famous dividend stock in history is a middling buy *at today's price*. Buffett's genius wasn't identifying that Coke pays reliable dividends — it was buying in 1988 at a price where that reliability now pays him over 60% a year on cost. A C+ at a 2.5% yield is the model telling you the easy part (knowing KO is reliable) is fully priced in, and the hard part (his entry) is not reproducible by buying today.

**Kraft Heinz, B at 6.6%.** The portfolio's great irony. The deal Buffett publicly called overpaid — "I was wrong in a couple of ways about Kraft Heinz" — has spent the years since cutting debt while its share price was crushed, and it now screens as the *best income stock Berkshire owns*: a B grade with a verified 6.6% yield, the only famous holding that would also make our [ultra-high-yield quality list](/blog/safest-ultra-high-yield-dividend-stocks). The punishment came first; the income investor arriving today inherits the cleaned-up balance sheet at the punished price.

## Why copying Buffett for income fails

Add it up: the famous holdings mostly yield 0.4–2.5%, the largest position yields 0.36%, one pays nothing, and the holding company itself has paid no dividend since 1967. **Berkshire's portfolio is a quality portfolio, not an income portfolio** — Buffett harvests cash through insurance float and wholly-owned businesses, not dividend checks. What *does* transfer is the filter: durable, cash-generating businesses bought when the price doesn't fully reflect it. That's a screen you can run today — it's roughly what our model's A-range list is — and right now it points at [different tickers](/blog/top-rated-dividend-stocks-june-2026) than Berkshire's 1988–2011 entry points do. Copy the method, not the museum.
