Is **Taylor Morrison**'s 22% jump justified? Yes — but for a simple reason: it's deal math, not a re-rating of the business. Taylor Morrison Home (NYSE: TMHC) was among the market's biggest movers on June 4, 2026, after a cash acquisition headline put a number on the stock.

## The move

TMHC jumped **+22.31% to $71.55**, valuing it at about **$6.7B** (52-week range **$51.90–$74.09**). There's no dividend, so this is purely an event-driven move.

## What's driving it

This is classic deal pricing:

- Multiple outlets referenced **Berkshire Hathaway's** proposed acquisition, with the key term: **$72.50 in cash per share** for TMHC (Business Wire, June 2; echoed by GuruFocus and PRNewsWire "investor alert" items).
- A Seeking Alpha comparative piece called out Berkshire's **$6.8B** acquisition and framed it as a signal that well-run homebuilders are attractively valued (June 3).
- The law-firm "investigates adequacy of price/process" headlines are routine in M&A and don't change the economics by themselves.

## The fundamentals

uncoverd rates TMHC **B+ (17/25)**: **Value 4/5** (cheapness is a feature), **Growth 3/5**, **Profitability 3/5**, **Health 4/5** (strong balance-sheet footing), and **Momentum 3/5**. In other words, a genuinely well-run, cheap homebuilder — exactly the profile a value buyer like Berkshire tends to target. ## Verdict: Fair

The stock is simply moving toward the **$72.50** cash consideration. With the price at **$71.55**, most of the remaining upside is merger spread, not business upside — and the "investigation" headlines add process noise, not a new catalyst.
